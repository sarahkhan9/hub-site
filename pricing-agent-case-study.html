The origin

This project didn't start with a market thesis. It started with a meeting.

In October 2024 I had an exploratory conversation with a fintech founder about a senior design role. Rather than preparing a slide deck, I built a live demo — an AI agent that could research any company's competitive pricing landscape, surface tier structures, and generate an actionable brief in under 30 seconds. I ran it on the founder's three closest competitors mid-conversation.

I didn't get the job. But the demo got forwarded three times before I'd left the building.

"The question stopped being 'can I impress an interviewer with this' and became 'why isn't this a product already?'"

Within a week I'd spoken to eleven people who'd seen the demo secondhand. Nine asked for access. Four offered to pay before there was anything to pay for. I paused the job search and started building.

How it works

The Pricing Model Agent takes a company name as input, live-searches its pricing pages and those of its competitors, then uses Claude to extract tier structures, identify positioning gaps, rate competitive threat levels, and generate a prioritized action brief — all in a single interface flow, no spreadsheet required.

The core design challenge was trust calibration. Pricing decisions carry real financial weight. If the agent returned confident-sounding output that was wrong, users wouldn't just lose trust in the tool — they'd lose trust in the category. That single insight drove most of the consequential design decisions that followed.

Challenges & resolutions

01
Critical — Week 2
The agent hallucinated with confidence

Early testing revealed the most dangerous failure mode: the agent returned pricing information that looked authoritative but was simply fabricated — a $49/month tier that didn't exist, a competitor described as freemium when they'd eliminated their free plan months earlier.

The problem wasn't accuracy alone. It was that inaccurate outputs were indistinguishable from accurate ones. One beta user adjusted their enterprise anchor price based on a fabricated competitor benchmark — and only discovered the error during a live sales call.

Resolution Built a live-search architecture — every data point is tagged with its source URL and retrieval timestamp. A confidence indicator (High / Verify / Stale) reflects data freshness, and users can click any claim to see the raw source. The agent became slower — 34 seconds vs 12 — but trust scores in testing jumped from 3.1 to 4.6 out of 5.
02
Growth blocker — Week 6
68% of trial users never completed a single run

Six weeks after launch, two-thirds of signups had never generated a brief. Qualitative interviews surfaced the issue fast: users didn't know what company to enter first. The blank input was perceived as a test they could fail.

"I wasn't sure if I should put in my own company or a competitor," one user said. "I didn't want to do it wrong." A blank text field shouldn't produce paralysis — but in a new category with no established mental model, it did.

Resolution Replaced the blank input with a guided first run: on signup, users answer two questions (their industry, their company size) and the agent auto-suggests three competitor names pre-populated in the search bar. First-run completion jumped from 32% to 81%. MoM growth accelerated from 14% to 22% in the two months following the change.
03
Revenue ceiling — Month 3
$149/mo was leaving money on the table

At month three, retention was strong (82% at 90 days) and several users mentioned they'd expected the tool to cost more. Two had explicitly recommended it to their boss on the basis that the ROI "seemed obvious at that price." Classic signals of underpricing.

But raising price on existing subscribers felt like breaking an implicit contract. And the sample size was too small for a clean A/B pricing test.

Resolution Introduced a second tier at $299/month — Slack delivery, weekly automated runs, CSV export — without raising the base price. Framing it as "built for teams" gave existing users a natural upgrade path rather than a forced migration. Within 45 days, 31% of existing subscribers had upgraded. Blended ARPU moved from $149 to $211.
04
Retention risk — Month 4
Power users ran the agent, then went dark for weeks

Usage logs showed a clear pattern: users ran the agent intensively during a pricing or planning cycle, then went dormant. The tool solved a point-in-time problem — and once it was solved, there was no reason to return until the next cycle, which might be months away.

Resolution Added a weekly market pulse digest — a lightweight Monday email summarizing any pricing changes detected across the user's tracked competitors. No user action required. 90-day retention recovered from 67% to 79% within two months of rollout, and it created a re-engagement trigger that brought dormant users back whenever a competitor moved.

What I learned

01
Trust is a design surface

In AI tools, the experience of confidence — knowing when to trust the output — matters as much as the output itself. Source attribution wasn't a feature. It was the product.

02
Blank inputs are a UX failure

Activation means removing every reason not to act. The best onboarding makes the first step feel inevitable, not optional. A blank field in a new category is an invitation to leave.

03
Pricing is a conversation

Adding a tier without raising the base price let users self-select their willingness to pay. ARPU growth came from understanding existing users — not acquiring new ones.

04
Retention needs a recurring need

The weekly digest turned a point-in-time tool into ambient intelligence. Passive delivery is often more durable than designing for active engagement.
